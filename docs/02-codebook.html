2  Codebook & Variable Dictionary

This Chapter documents every utilized variable in the analysis dataset, including response scales, coding schemes, naming conventions, and treatment conditions. Additional variables not used in current analysis are also described. It serves as the authoritative reference for interpreting the data.

2.1 Data File

A single canonical CSV contains all observations:

This file was produced by cleaning the original Qualtrics export: correcting UTF-8 encoding artifacts (smart quotes rendered as mojibake in the original export) and removing 17 rows with invalid GROUP values (empty strings, zeros, and text spillover from unescaped CSV fields). The original working files (ccSurveyWorkingFile3.csv and ccSurveyWorkingFileB1.csv) are preserved in data/survey-data/ for provenance but are not used by this analysis. The raw Qualtrics export is also available on OSF.io but not used directly in this analysis. It does not contain personal identifiers.

2.1.2 The B1 Supplement

Group B1 is a supplemental data collection of 97 participants who replaced Group B for the Educational Use (EU) scenario. An error in the randomized distribution excluded B group participants from seeing the EU scenario. B1 participants completed only the EU portion of the survey; their non-EU scenario columns (PD, PU, CA, LS, WN, FS) are empty. B1 participants also did not complete the brand recognition battery (logo identification, NETSKILLS, BRANDSKILLS) or the demographic questions — these columns are NA for all B1 rows. Group B1 data differs from Group B in two ways: (1) B1 participants were recruited after the main data collection and may differ demographically from the original B group; (2) B1 participants were not exposed to the non-EU scenarios, which may have influenced their responses to the EU scenario. For these reasons, Group B1 is treated as a separate group in the EU scenario analysis (replacing the missing Group B data) and excluded from all other analyses.

For the EU scenario analysis (§7.3), the data is filtered to Groups A, B1, and C — Group B is excluded entirely. For all other scenarios, the data is filtered to Groups A, B, and C — Group B1 is excluded (since B1 participants did not answer those questions).

2.1.3 Data Collection Timeline

All data was collected in March 2024 via CloudResearch’s Connect platform, with the survey administered through Qualtrics.1

1 For a detailed discussion of the platform selection, pilot testing, and data quality measures, see the dissertation chapter: Thomas H. Rousse, Hidden Costs of Open Licensing? A Survey Experiment on Creative Commons and Copyright Infringement, ch. 1 in Creative Realism: Innovation Beyond the Ownership Incentive (2024), Part V.A.

Demographic quotas on age, gender, race, and ethnicity were used to approximate national representativeness. Sampling lasted roughly 16 hours for the main collection, with responses distributed in two large groups on the evening of March 14 and morning of March 15. The median completion time was 13:58, with an average of 16:50. For detailed sample composition and population comparisons, see Chapter 5 (Sample v. Population Analysis).

2.3 Variable Naming Conventions

Variables follow a consistent naming scheme. Understanding the pattern is essential for navigating the 191 columns in the dataset.

2.3.1 Scenario–Question Pattern

The core variables use a [SCENARIO][QUESTION] pattern:

Scenario codes:

Question suffixes:

Example: PD1 = Public Domain, Q1 (perceived likelihood of legal consequences). EU25 = Educational Use, Delta (perceived law minus preferred rule).

2.3.2 Treatment-Specific Columns

The dataset preserves responses in treatment-specific columns before merging:

Each participant has data in only one treatment column set (C, TA, or TB) based on their random assignment. The merged columns (e.g., PD1) contain the same value copied from the appropriate treatment column for that participant.

2.3.3 Timing Columns

Per-scenario page timing follows the pattern [SCENARIO][CONDITION]-TIME_Page Submit (e.g., PDC-TIME_Page Submit, PUTA-TIME_Page Submit). These record the time in seconds each participant spent on the corresponding scenario page.

2.4 Response Scales

2.4.1 Q1: Likelihood of Legal Consequences (1–7 Likert)

“On a scale from 1–7, with 7 being the most likely: In your opinion, how likely is [the re-user] to face legal consequences for this use?”

2.4.2 Q2: Perceived Legal Consequences (1–7 Likert)

“On a scale from 1–7, with 7 being the most negative: In your opinion, if [the re-user] was sued for these actions, what legal consequences do you think they would face?”

2.4.3 Q3: Preferred Legal Consequences (1–7 Likert)

“On a scale from 1–7, with 7 being the most negative: In your opinion, what legal consequences should [the re-user] face for these actions, if sued?”

The response scale is identical to Q2 (see above). Q2 asks what would happen; Q3 asks what should happen.

2.4.4 Delta (Q2 − Q3): Congruence Score

The Delta variable ([SCENARIO]25) is computed as Q2 minus Q3 for each scenario. Range: −6 to +6 (integer). See Chapter 1, §1.4.2 for the full definition and interpretation.

2.4.5 Q4: Reasonable License Price (Continuous)

“Now imagine that before the use described in the scenario, [the re-user] and the owner of this work agreed on a price for a non-exclusive license — meaning that the owner can also license the work to others — that allows all of the use described in the scenario. In your opinion, what do you think would be a reasonable price for that license, if any? Please provide a single amount in US dollars.”

  • Type: Text entry (numeric)
  • Validation: Numeric values only, minimum $0, two decimal places
  • Unit: US dollars

2.4.6 NETSKILLS / BRANDSKILLS (1–5 Matrix)

This scale is used for both the Internet Skills battery (NETSKILLS) and the Brand Familiarity battery (BRANDSKILLS). See Brand Recognition & Internet Skills for item details.

2.5 Demographic Variables

All demographic questions were presented after the scenarios. The question formats follow long-standing U.S. Census Survey conventions where applicable. To reduce variance based on question language changes, archaic language reflecting government practice from decades past is sometimes retained.

2.5.1 AGE

  • Question: “What is your current age?”
  • Type: Text entry (numeric)
  • Scale: Continuous (integer years)
  • CSV column: AGE

2.5.2 GENDER

  • Question: “With what gender do you most closely identify?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer
  • CSV column: GENDER (numeric code); GENDER_3_TEXT (free-text for option 3)

2.5.3 HISPLAT

  • Question: “Are you of Spanish, Hispanic, or Latino origin?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer
  • CSV column: HISPLAT

2.5.4 RACE

  • Question: “Choose one or more races that you consider yourself to be”
  • Type: Multiple choice, multiple answer
  • CSV column: RACE
  • Encoding: Comma-separated string of selected values (e.g., "1,4" = White and Asian). Single-race responses are stored as a single integer (e.g., "1").

2.5.5 EDU

  • Question: “What is the highest level of education you have completed?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer (ordinal)
  • CSV column: EDU

2.5.6 INCOME

  • Question: “What was your total household income before taxes during the past 12 months?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer (ordinal)
  • CSV column: INCOME

2.5.7 STATE

  • Question: “In what state or territory do you currently live?”
  • Type: Dropdown menu
  • CSV column: STATE
  • Values: US state two-letter abbreviations (e.g., “AL”, “CA”, “NY”), plus “Other” and “Outside US”
NoteRecoded from Qualtrics Export

The original Qualtrics export used non-sequential internal choice IDs (integers 4–115). These have been recoded to standard two-letter state abbreviations. The original numeric codes are preserved in the archived CSV at data/survey-data/prev/.

2.5.8 LIBCON

  • Question: “Politically, do you tend to be more liberal, or conservative?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer (horizontal display)
  • CSV column: LIBCON
NoteRecoded from Qualtrics Export

The original Qualtrics export used non-sequential raw codes (1, 2, 6, 7, 3) due to the “Moderate” option being inserted after initial scale construction. These have been recoded to a sequential 1–5 ordinal scale. The original CSV is archived at data/survey-data/prev/rousse-cc-survey-2024_pre-libcon-recode.csv.

2.5.9 POLPARTY

  • Question: “What political party, if any, do you most closely affiliate with?”
  • Type: Multiple choice, single answer
  • CSV column: POLPARTY (numeric code); POLPARTY_4_TEXT (free-text for option 4)

2.6 Brand Recognition & Internet Skills

2.6.1 Logo Identification Battery

Participants were shown four logos in random order and asked: “Do you know the brand associated with this logo?” Those who answered “Yes” for IDCC were prompted: “Please identify the brand you associate with this logo.”

  • Response coding: 1 = Yes (recognized), 2 = No (not recognized)
  • Text columns: IDMcD-Text, IDFAKE-Text, IDFF-Text, IDCC-Text — free-text brand identification

2.6.2 IDCC-CAT: CC Logo Response Categorization

Free-text responses to the CC logo identification (IDCC-Text) were hand-coded into the following categories:

2.6.3 CCREC: CC Knowledge Description

  • Question: “You indicated you recognize Creative Commons. Please briefly describe in your own words your understanding of Creative Commons. Please do not use AI assistance or outside sources.”
  • Type: Free-text entry
  • Condition: Shown only to participants who answered “Yes” to IDCC
  • CSV column: CCREC

2.6.4 Internet Skills (NETSKILLS)

“How familiar are you with the following computer and Internet-related items? Please choose a number between 1 and 5 where 1 represents ‘no understanding’ and 5 represents ‘full understanding’ of the item.”2

2 Measure from Eszter Hargittai & Yuli Patrick Hsieh, Succinct Survey Measures of Web-Use Skills, 30 Soc. Sci. Computer Rev. 95 (2012). Items in this and the following BRANDSKILLS battery were presented randomly to preclude order effects.

Scale: 1–5 (None / Little / Some / Good / Full). See §2.6.

2.6.5 Brand Familiarity (BRANDSKILLS)

“How familiar are you with the following computer and Internet-related brands? Please choose a number between 1 and 5 where 1 represents ‘no understanding’ and 5 represents ‘full understanding’ of the item.”3

3 Follows format of Eszter Hargittai & Yuli Patrick Hsieh, Succinct Survey Measures of Web-Use Skills, 30 Soc. Sci. Computer Rev. 95 (2012).

Scale: 1–5 (None / Little / Some / Good / Full). See §2.6.

2.7 Quality Control & Attention Checks

Three quality-control items were administered during the survey. These were used as screening questions — participants who failed attention/consent checks were excluded during data collection. The responses are not retained in the analysis dataset.

2.7.1 COMMIT (Commitment Question)

“We care about your opinions and we care about the quality of our survey data. To get the most accurate measures of your opinions, it is important that you provide thoughtful answers in good faith to each question in this survey. Please do not use text generated by AI or LLMs such as ChatGPT. Your answers to questions about your personal opinions will have no impact on your compensation. Do you commit to providing thoughtful answers in good faith to the questions in the survey?”

2.7.2 PINK (Color Attention Check)

“Which of the following colors is a lighter shade of red?”

Options: Green, Pink (correct), Yellow, Black, Blue.

2.7.3 FIRSTNAMEGEN (Name Attention Check)

“Which of the following names do you think is most likely to belong to someone who identifies as a female?”

Options: Henry, Robert, Elizabeth (correct), Timothy, Edward.

2.7.4 Feedback

  • CSV column: Feedback
  • Type: Optional free-text
  • Content: Open-ended participant feedback collected at the end of the survey

### The B1 Supplement {#sec-b1}

Group B1 is a **supplemental data collection** of 97 participants who replaced Group B for the Educational Use (EU) scenario. An error in the randomized distribution excluded B group participants from seeing the EU scenario. B1 participants completed only the EU portion of the survey; their non-EU scenario columns (PD, PU, CA, LS, WN, FS) are empty. B1 participants also did not complete the brand recognition battery (logo identification, NETSKILLS, BRANDSKILLS) or the demographic questions — these columns are NA for all B1 rows. Group B1 data differs from Group B in two ways: (1) B1 participants were recruited after the main data collection and may differ demographically from the original B group; (2) B1 participants were not exposed to the non-EU scenarios, which may have influenced their responses to the EU scenario. For these reasons, Group B1 is treated as a separate group in the EU scenario analysis (replacing the missing Group B data) and excluded from all other analyses.

For the EU scenario analysis ([§7.3](07-scenarios.qmd#sec-eu)), the data is filtered to Groups A, B1, and C — Group B is excluded entirely. For all other scenarios, the data is filtered to Groups A, B, and C — Group B1 is excluded (since B1 participants did not answer those questions).

## Variable Naming Conventions {#sec-naming}

Variables follow a consistent naming scheme. Understanding the pattern is essential for navigating the 191 columns in the dataset.

### Scenario–Question Pattern

The core variables use a `[SCENARIO][QUESTION]` pattern:

**Scenario codes:**

| Code | Scenario |
|------|----------|
| `PD` | Public Domain |
| `PU` | Personal Use |
| `EU` | Educational Use |
| `CA` | Commercial Advertising |
| `LS` | Large Scale Copying |
| `WN` | Wikipedia Image Search |
| `FS` | Filesharing |

: Scenario Codes. {#tbl-scenario-codes}

**Question suffixes:**

| Suffix | Meaning | Scale |
|--------|---------|-------|
| `1` | Likelihood of legal consequences (Q1) | 1–7 Likert |
| `2` | What legal consequences would they face? (Q2) | 1–7 Likert |
| `3` | What legal consequences should they face? (Q3) | 1–7 Likert |
| `4` | Reasonable license price (Q4) | Continuous (USD) |
| `25` | Delta = Q2 − Q3 (computed) | −6 to +6 |

: Question Suffixes. {#tbl-question-suffixes tbl-colwidths="[10,60,30]"}

**Example:** `PD1` = Public Domain, Q1 (perceived likelihood of legal consequences). `EU25` = Educational Use, Delta (perceived law minus preferred rule).

### Treatment-Specific Columns

The dataset preserves responses in **treatment-specific columns** before merging:

| Pattern | Meaning | Example |
|---------|---------|---------|
| `[SCENARIO]C[1-4]` | Control group response | `PDC1`, `PUC3` |
| `[SCENARIO]TA[1-4]` | Short (A) response | `PDTA1`, `PUTA4` |
| `[SCENARIO]TB[1-4]` | Full + Primer (B) response | `PDTB2`, `PUTB3` |
| `[SCENARIO][1-4]` | Merged column (aggregated across conditions) | `PD1`, `PU3` |

: Treatment-Specific Column Patterns. {#tbl-treatment-columns}

Each participant has data in only one treatment column set (C, TA, or TB) based on their random assignment. The merged columns (e.g., `PD1`) contain the same value copied from the appropriate treatment column for that participant.

### Timing Columns

Per-scenario page timing follows the pattern `[SCENARIO][CONDITION]-TIME_Page Submit` (e.g., `PDC-TIME_Page Submit`, `PUTA-TIME_Page Submit`). These record the time in seconds each participant spent on the corresponding scenario page.

In your opinion, what do you think would be a reasonable price for that license, if any? Please provide a single amount in US dollars."

      Variable %in% c("AGE", "Duration (in seconds)") ~ "Continuous",
      grepl("TIME|Page Submit", Variable) ~ "Continuous",
      grepl("^(PD|PU|EU|CA|LS|WN|FS).*4$", Variable) ~ "Continuous (USD)",
      grepl("25$", Variable) ~ "Integer (computed)",
      grepl("clicked$", Variable) ~ "Binary (0/1)",
      grepl("^(PD|PU|EU|CA|LS|WN|FS)", Variable) ~ "Ordinal (1-7)",
      grepl("^NETSKILLS_[1-6]|^BRANDSKILLS", Variable) ~ "Ordinal (1-5)",
      Variable == "NETSKILLS_AVG" ~ "Continuous",
      Variable %in% c("participantId", "GROUP") ~ "Categorical",
      Variable %in% c("GENDER", "HISPLAT", "RACE", "EDU", "INCOME",
                       "LIBCON", "POLPARTY") ~ "Categorical",
      grepl("^ID(McD|FAKE|FF|CC)$", Variable) ~ "Binary (1=Yes/2=No)",
      Variable == "IDCC-CAT" ~ "Categorical (1-6)",
      TRUE ~ "—"
    ),
    N_Valid = sapply(Variable, function(v) sum(!is.na(survey_all[[v]]))),
    Pct_Missing = round(100 * (nrow(survey_all) - N_Valid) / nrow(survey_all), 1)
